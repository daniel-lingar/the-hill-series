# Episode 7 — The Marriage
## Act 5 — A Public Record

**Status:** GAP CLOSED — FIRST FULL SCRIPT PASS

**Evidence Lane:** Marriage of John Samuel Acord and Amanda Catherine Hill is the structural historical anchor. Ceremony details are dramatized.

---

### SCENE 1 — EXT. COUNTRY CHURCH / YARD — MORNING

The wedding morning does not look like peace.

It looks like wagons in grass. Men standing too far apart. Women carrying food inside with the stiff posture of people who know every eye is measuring the room.

The church is plain. White boards weathered by heat and rain. A bell that sounds smaller than the day requires.

JOHN SAMUEL ACORD stands near the fence in his best coat, which is not new. His hands are clean, but the nails still remember work.

CHRISTOPHER stands beside him.

CHRISTOPHER
You can still leave.

JOHN SAMUEL
That supposed to be comfort?

CHRISTOPHER
No. Just checking if you know.

John Samuel almost smiles.

JOHN SAMUEL
I know.

Across the yard, AMANDA CATHERINE HILL steps down from a wagon.

She is not lit like rescue. She is dressed simply. Hair pinned. Face steady. A woman arriving as herself, not as symbol.

Whispers move before she reaches the door.

HILL.

ACORD.

HORNER.

Some whispers are warning. Some are curiosity. Some are old fear trying to find new work.

Amanda hears them. She keeps walking.

---

### SCENE 2 — INT. CHURCH — BEFORE CEREMONY

Families fill benches with gaps between them that matter more than empty wood should.

Mary Ann sits near the front. Permelia beside her. Older now, but not softened into harmlessness.

A HILL MAN sits on the other side, face guarded.

Amanda stands in the small back room, looking at her hands.

Her MOTHER adjusts a seam.

AMANDA'S MOTHER
You don’t have to carry all their looking.

AMANDA
They’re not looking at me. Not all the way.

AMANDA'S MOTHER
Then make them.

Amanda absorbs that.

Outside the back room, John Samuel waits near the door. He does not enter.

JOHN SAMUEL
Amanda?

AMANDA
If you are here to ask if I’m sure, I will come out and embarrass you before God and both families.

JOHN SAMUEL
I’m not asking that.

AMANDA
Good.

JOHN SAMUEL
I’m asking if you want more time.

Amanda opens the door.

AMANDA
Time for what?

JOHN SAMUEL
For everybody to become better than they are.

Amanda looks past him toward the sanctuary.

AMANDA
Then we would die unmarried.

That lands. They both know it is true.

AMANDA (CONT'D)
I am not marrying them.

JOHN SAMUEL
They will still sit in the room.

AMANDA
Then let the room learn.

---

### SCENE 3 — INT. CHURCH — CEREMONY

The preacher stands with a Bible worn soft at the edges.

John Samuel and Amanda face each other.

The room is too quiet. Not reverent. Braced.

PREACHER
We are gathered—

A baby fusses. Someone coughs. Life refuses to be theatrical.

The preacher continues.

PREACHER (CONT'D)
—to join this man and this woman in lawful marriage.

He looks at the room as much as at the couple.

PREACHER (CONT'D)
Marriage is not the erasing of names. It is the making of a house where truth must be spoken without burning the table down every night.

Some people shift. Others look down.

PREACHER (CONT'D)
John Samuel Acord, do you take Amanda Catherine Hill to be your wife?

John Samuel looks at Amanda. Not at the room.

JOHN SAMUEL
I do.

He stops.

The preacher waits.

JOHN SAMUEL (CONT'D)
And I will answer for it.

Mary Ann closes her eyes.

PREACHER
Amanda Catherine Hill, do you take John Samuel Acord to be your husband?

Amanda’s voice is clear.

AMANDA
I do.

She looks at John Samuel, then at the room.

AMANDA (CONT'D)
And I will not disappear inside it.

The line unsettles the room because it is not soft.

The preacher nods as if he approves of vows that know how to stand.

PREACHER
Then before God and these witnesses, what is joined here is not a forgetting. It is a keeping of covenant among the living.

They are pronounced married.

No thunder. No healing music. Just breath returning to bodies one at a time.

---

### SCENE 4 — EXT. CHURCH YARD — AFTER CEREMONY

Food appears because people who do not know what to say still know how to set out dishes.

Amanda and John Samuel stand near the fence.

Mary Ann approaches with a folded cloth.

Not fancy. Mended. Clean. Old.

MARY ANN
This was on our table before the war broke the house into before and after.

Amanda looks at it, understanding the weight.

AMANDA
I don’t know if I should take that.

MARY ANN
You should not take it as proof you fixed anything.

Amanda nods.

MARY ANN (CONT'D)
You do not have to fix what you did not break. But you will live near the cracks.

Amanda takes the cloth with both hands.

AMANDA
Then I will not pretend I don’t see them.

Mary Ann studies her. For the first time, something like approval passes between them.

MARY ANN
That is more than many do.

---

### SCENE 5 — EXT. CHURCH YARD / FOOD TABLE — LATER

The table becomes the test.

Horner-Acord side. Hill side. Neighbors. Children running between legs because children do not understand invisible borders until adults teach them.

A Hill man reaches for a plate at the same time as Christopher.

Both stop.

The Hill man gestures.

HILL MAN
Go ahead.

Christopher takes the plate, then stops.

CHRISTOPHER
You knew my people?

The table quiets.

John Samuel starts to move, but Amanda touches his wrist. Wait.

The Hill man looks at Christopher.

HILL MAN
Some.

CHRISTOPHER
Were you against them?

That is a child’s question. Too blunt for adult cowardice.

The Hill man breathes in.

HILL MAN
Sometimes our people stood on different sides. Sometimes they stood in the same mud and called it different names. I was not at your house.

Christopher studies him.

CHRISTOPHER
That ain’t all I asked.

The Hill man nods.

HILL MAN
No. It ain’t.

A long silence.

PERMELIA
Let the table learn.

The words free just enough air for plates to move again.

Not forgiveness. Not settlement. Practice.

---

### SCENE 6 — EXT. FENCE LINE — SUNSET

Amanda and John Samuel stand where their Act Four promise began.

The wedding is behind them now: voices, wagon wheels, dishes, children.

John Samuel holds the old paper he has kept for years.

HOUSE.

CAVE.

ROAD.

BELIEVE.

Amanda sees it.

AMANDA
You brought him?

JOHN SAMUEL
I bring him everywhere.

Amanda does not tell him to put it away.

AMANDA
Then we better make room without letting him choose every chair.

John Samuel looks at her.

JOHN SAMUEL
I don’t know how.

AMANDA
Neither do I.

That is honest enough to begin.

She takes his hand.

AMANDA (CONT'D)
Do not throw away the boy to become a husband. Just do not make me live inside his cave.

John Samuel folds the paper carefully.

JOHN SAMUEL
I can try.

AMANDA
Trying is not a vow if you use it as an excuse.

He looks at her, then laughs once — surprised, real.

JOHN SAMUEL
You are not peace.

AMANDA
I told you.

JOHN SAMUEL
You are Amanda.

She smiles faintly.

AMANDA
Good. Remember that when the dead get loud.

---

### SCENE 7 — MODERN FRAME / ARCHIVAL RECORD — VOICEOVER

A marriage record appears.

Names. Date. County. Ink doing what ink does: making a clean line out of a life that was not clean.

DANIEL / BRET (V.O.)
The record says they married. It does not say what the room cost. It does not say who swallowed anger, who carried suspicion, who came anyway, who stayed away, who watched a Hill woman take an Acord name and decided whether that was betrayal, courage, or none of their business.

Images overlap: the table cloth; the fence; Amanda’s hand on the old paper; Mary Ann watching from the church yard.

DANIEL / BRET (V.O.)
The war ended on paper. The family continued in flesh.

John Samuel and Amanda walk away from the fence toward the table.

Not toward peace.

Toward work.

The camera holds on the table as someone places an extra cup beside the mended cloth.

Blackout.

**END ACT FIVE.**
