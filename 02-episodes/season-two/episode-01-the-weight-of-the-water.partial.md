## FADE IN:

### TEASER

### EXT. CREEK — PAST (1866) — MORNING

The sun is barely up, painting the sky in soft oranges and purples. AMANDA (30s), her face etched with a quiet weariness that wasn't there before the war, kneels by a creek. She scrubs clothes against a washboard, the rhythmic scrape a stark contrast to the profound silence of the woods around her.

The water runs clear, reflecting the nascent light. Suddenly, a disturbance upstream. The reflection shatters. The water begins to cloud, turning a murky brown. Amanda doesn't look up immediately. Her movements slow, but she doesn't stop. She knows this pattern.

A moment later, the sound of hooves. A lone RIDER, silhouetted against the rising sun, crosses the creek further up. He doesn't look at Amanda. He doesn't need to. His presence is enough. He's just a man on a horse, but in this landscape, in this time, he carries the weight of a thousand unspoken threats.

Amanda watches him disappear into the trees. Her hands, still in the now-muddy water, clench. The war may be over, but the fear, the constant vigilance, is a habit that refuses to die.

### INT. MODERN DANIEL'S ROOM — NIGHT

The room is quiet, bathed in the cool glow of a laptop screen. DANIEL (40s) sits at his desk. The handmade evidence system on the wall is still there, but it's no longer a frantic web of questions. It's an organized archive. The labels are neat: PROVEN, SUPPORTED, TRADITION, QUESTION, UNKNOWN, BOUNDARIES, LIVING SOURCES.

On his laptop, two windows are open. One is the empty document titled "THE_HILL_ACCOUNT_NOT_AVENGE.docx." The other is the now-deleted "THE_HILL_REVENGE_CUT.docx" in his recycling bin, a ghost of a choice made. He stares at the blank page, the cursor blinking, a tiny, insistent pulse in the vast emptiness.

Choosing the truth, deleting the easy lie, was the climax of Season One. Writing it, living it, is the work of Season Two. And the work, Daniel realizes, is often quieter, heavier, than the fight.

FADE OUT.

## ACT ONE

### INT. MODERN DANIEL'S ROOM — MORNING

Sunlight streams through the window, illuminating dust motes dancing in the air. Daniel is asleep at his desk, head resting on his arms, the laptop still open. A soft PING from the laptop startles him awake. He blinks, disoriented, then focuses on the screen.

An email notification. From: "Sonja and Becky." Subject: "Re: Hill family research question from a cousin."

Daniel's breath hitches. This is it. The moment he's been bracing for. He clicks it open. The email is short, formal, and carefully worded.

*Daniel,  
We received your email. We appreciate your thoroughness. We have always understood the family history in a particular way, passed down through generations. It is deeply ingrained. If you have new information, we are willing to review it. However, we ask that you approach this with respect for what has come before. Do not seek to erase the memory of those we hold dear, even if your findings differ.  
Sonja and Becky.*

Daniel rereads it. Not the angry rejection he'd feared. But not a warm embrace either. It's defensive, cautious, a new kind of boundary. The living, he realizes, are often harder to navigate than the dead. They have their own stories, their own wounds.

### EXT. JOHN SAMUEL'S FARM — PAST (1866) — DAY

JOHN SAMUEL (30s), lean and weathered, is mending a split rail fence. The work is slow, methodical. The sounds of hammers and saws are scarce in this post-war landscape. He works in silence, his movements economical.

In the distance, a cloud of dust. A horse and rider approach along the dirt road. John Samuel doesn't stop working, but his posture stiffens, his eyes tracking the approaching figure. It's a NEIGHBOR, a man he knows fought for the Confederacy. John Samuel, of course, fought for the Union.

The Neighbor rides past, slowing slightly. Their eyes meet. No words are exchanged. Just a brief, tense nod. It's the most fragile peace imaginable, a silent agreement to coexist on land still scarred by conflict. The air crackles with unspoken history, with the memory of battles fought and loyalties divided. The peace is not a given; it is a constant, deliberate act.

FADE OUT.

## ACT TWO

### INT. MODERN DANIEL'S KITCHEN — MORNING

Daniel sits at the kitchen table with his four children: MAVERICK (12), SHAYLA (10), TEAGUEN (8), and ADALEE (6). Cereal bowls and milk cartons are scattered across the table. Daniel holds his phone, showing them the email from Sonja and Becky.

**DANIEL**  
(Quietly)  
So, they replied.

Maverick, ever direct, spoons a mouthful of cereal.

**MAVERICK**  
So, do we fight them?

Daniel smiles, a sad, knowing smile.

**DANIEL**  
No, Mav. We don't fight them. We show them the table.

He gestures vaguely towards the living room, where his evidence wall is. The kids look at him, confused.

**DANIEL (CONT'D)**  
We built a table in that room. A place where claims come before they become weapons. Now, we have to invite people to sit at it. Even if they don't want to.

He closes the email. The work, he realizes, is no longer just about discovery. It's about presentation. It's about making the "Account" accessible, undeniable, and, most importantly, non-confrontational. He begins to mentally organize the records, not for his own understanding, but for sharing.

### EXT. WILLIAM RILEY'S GRAVE — PAST (1867) — DAY

MARY ANN (60s), older now, her hair streaked with more gray, stands before WILLIAM RILEY HORNER'S grave. The stone is no longer fresh, but still relatively new. The inscription, "KILLED BY PARTIES KNOWN TO HIS FAMILY," is clear.

She reaches out, her fingers tracing the carved letters. Her face is a mask of profound grief and resignation. She whispers, almost to herself, almost to William.

**MARY ANN**  
It's enough.

But her eyes, deep and haunted, tell a different story. The words on the stone are a boundary, a carefully chosen truth that prevents further bloodshed. But holding that boundary, living with that partial truth, has taken its toll. It's a peace bought at a great cost, a constant ache in her soul.

FADE OUT.

## ACT THREE

### INT. LOCAL LIBRARY — MODERN — DAY

Daniel walks into a quiet, sunlit local library. He carries a worn leather binder, thick with printed documents and notes. He approaches the local history section, where an ARCHIVIST (50s, kind, bespectacled) is meticulously cataloging old photographs.

**DANIEL**  
Excuse me. I'm Daniel Lingar. I've been doing some research on the Hill and Horner families.

**ARCHIVIST**  
Ah, yes. A fascinating, if sometimes contentious, local history. What can I help you with?

**DANIEL**  
I'd like to submit some findings. A corrected, more nuanced account, I hope. For the public record.

He places the binder on the counter. The Archivist looks at the binder, then at Daniel, a flicker of understanding in her eyes. She's seen this before. The weight of family history, the desire to set the record straight.

**ARCHIVIST**  
(Softly)  
The past is rarely as simple as we want it to be.

**DANIEL**  
No. It's not. But it's worth trying to get it right.

The Archivist nods, a respectful acknowledgment. She opens the binder, her fingers carefully turning the pages. It feels anticlimactic, this quiet act of submission in a hushed library. But for Daniel, it is monumental. It is the public declaration of his choice, the first step in sharing the "Account" beyond his own wall.

### INT. JOHN SAMUEL AND AMANDA'S CABIN — PAST (1867) — NIGHT

Amanda and John Samuel sit at their small, mended dinner table. The leg, once wobbly, now holds steady, thanks to John Samuel's careful wedge. A single lantern casts long shadows across the room. They eat a simple meal in silence. The kind of silence that isn't empty, but full of shared understanding, of hardships endured and a future uncertain.

It's a hard peace they've found, born of necessity and resilience. The war has taken so much, but it hasn't taken their ability to build, to mend, to sit together at a table that holds steady, even if only a little.

FADE OUT.

## ACT FOUR

### EXT. LIBRARY — MODERN — DAY

Daniel walks out of the library, the empty binder now light in his hand. The sun is bright, the air fresh. He takes a deep breath. The weight isn't gone, but it has shifted. It's no longer a burden he carries alone; it's a contribution he's made.

### EXT. CREEK — PAST (1867) — DAY

Amanda walks back from the creek, a basket of clean, wet clothes balanced on her hip. The water runs clear today. The sun is high. She looks out at the fields, at the cabin, at the life they are painstakingly rebuilding.
